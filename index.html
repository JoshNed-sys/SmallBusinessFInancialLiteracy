Module 1 of 7

How lending has changed

Your credit score isn't the whole story anymore. Here's what actually matters now.

🔄

The old way vs. the new way

Lenders used to ask: do you have good credit and collateral? They now ask: does your bank account show a business that can repay this loan? These are different questions.

Old rules

Credit score + collateral

Bring a tax return. Have a good FICO. Put something up if you can't pay. That was the playbook.

New rules

Real-time cash flow

Your bank account is now your most important financial document. Lenders want to see how money actually moves through your business.

"The industry has moved from looking at static documents to understanding the actual health of a business in real time. Cash flow tells you what's happening — not just what happened."

— From Ned's lender conversations
What this means for you

A good credit score still helps. But 12-24 months of clean, consistent bank history is now the most powerful thing you can bring to a loan conversation.

Module 2 of 7

What lenders actually look at

6 signals. Know which ones can kill a deal before you apply.

Deal killer

NSFs (bounced payments)

Even 2–3 in the past 6 months can stop a deal. They signal you're spending more than you have.

Deal killer

Commingling accounts

Personal and business money in the same account. Lenders need a clean picture. This makes it impossible.

Matters a lot

Cash flow consistency

$8k/month every month beats $2k one month and $22k the next — even if the average is the same.

Matters a lot

Revenue trajectory

Growing slowly is better than declining. A lender bets on where you're going, not just where you are.

Matters a lot

Repayment headroom

After expenses and existing debt, do you have enough left to make loan payments? Lenders calculate this.

Trust signal

Willingness to share data

Borrowers who connect accounts and share information openly signal they have nothing to hide.

"If they've had five NSFs in the last six months, we're done. If they're commingling funds, we're also done."

— Ulric Donawa, BCL of Texas
Module 3 of 7

Read your own cash flow

Lenders run these numbers before you sit down. Run them first.

The most important calculation — DSCR
Monthly income after expenses ÷ Monthly debt payments

A result above 1.25 means you can cover your debts and still have 25% left over. That's the threshold most lenders want to see.

1
Average daily balance

How much is usually sitting in your account day to day? A consistently low average signals you're running thin.

2
Monthly income pattern

Is it consistent or swinging? Lenders look at 12–24 months to find your "normal."

3
Free cash flow

After all expenses — what's left? That's what a loan gets sized against. It's why you often get approved for less than you asked for.

If your business is seasonal

Say it first. "Revenue drops 40% in January every year but recovers by March." That's completely fine — unexplained volatility is the problem, not the seasonality itself.

Module 4 of 7

Before you apply

Check everything off before submitting. Most applications fail on things that could have been fixed first.

  • Business and personal accounts are completely separate
  • At least 12 months of bank history (24 is better)
  • No NSFs in the past 6 months — or can explain them
  • Know your monthly revenue number from memory — not just from a document
  • Know your existing debts — lenders will find them
  • Have a one-paragraph "use of proceeds" — specific, not vague
  • Know your legal structure, EIN, and registration status

"When you say, send me your financial statement, they send you their bank statement. That's not a character flaw — it's a gap we can fill."

— Todd Buchanan, Texas Capital
Module 5 of 7

What happens during underwriting

What's actually going on after you hit submit.

1
Someone writes your story

A loan officer writes a "credit memo" summarizing your business and recommending yes or no. Make their job easy — be organized, responsive, and clear about what the money is for.

2
A committee decides

At most CDFIs, a small group reviews and votes. They're reading the credit memo — not your original application. The better your loan officer understands your business, the better that memo is.

3
They may come back with a smaller number

Not a rejection — a sizing decision. They calculated your free cash flow and sized the loan to what you can actually repay. You can accept, negotiate, or build a track record for more later.

Kills deals

NSFs, commingling, declining revenue

Visible from the first review of your bank data.

Speeds things up

Organized, responsive, specific

Know your numbers. Respond fast. Be clear about what the money is for.

Module 6 of 7

How to present yourself

How you talk about your business matters as much as the numbers.

🎙️

The 60-second pitch

Practice: what you do, who you serve, how long you've been doing it, what the money is for. "I run a cleaning business in Houston, 6 years, 40 regular clients. I need $25k to hire a second employee for the commercial clients I've been turning away."

💬
Address rough patches first

"In early 2023, my biggest client cancelled — you'll see a dip for 4 months. I replaced that revenue by fall." Context turns a red flag into a story. Don't wait for them to find it.

📄
When you don't have something they asked for

Don't disappear. Respond quickly with what you do have. Responsiveness signals trustworthiness even when the documents aren't perfect.

❓
Ask good questions

"What would make this a stronger application?" "Is there anything that concerns you?" Good borrowers ask questions. It shows you understand this is a relationship, not a transaction.

The follow-up rule

Follow up once a week if you haven't heard anything. "Just checking if there's anything else you need from me." Once is professional. Twice in a day is not.

Module 7 of 7

After the loan

How you behave after funding determines whether the next loan is easier or harder.

1
Lenders often keep watching

Many CDFIs maintain a relationship post-funding. Respond to check-ins promptly. Proactively share updates if something changes.

2
Call them before they call you

If you're going to miss a payment or hit a rough patch — tell them first. Lenders work with borrowers who communicate. They don't work with borrowers who disappear.

3
Every payment builds your track record

Your next loan will be easier — and likely larger, with better terms — if you handle this one well. The foundation is built right now.

🏗️

Keep your accounts clean — permanently

No commingling. No NSFs. Consistent income. Clean books. These aren't just pre-loan habits — they're what a business with permanent access to capital looks like.
