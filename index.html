How Lending Has Changed

The shift from credit & collateral to cash flow — and why it matters for you

If you've tried to get a small business loan in the last few years and come away confused — or rejected — there's a good chance it's not because you did something wrong. It's because the rules changed, and most borrowers haven't been told.

Here's what changed: for a long time, getting a small business loan meant having good credit, collateral (something to put up if you couldn't pay), and a clean tax return. Lenders used these because they were the best proxies they had for whether you'd pay them back.

That's no longer the full picture. Especially for working capital loans under $250,000 — the range most small businesses actually need — lenders have shifted to looking at your real-time bank data. They want to see how money actually moves in and out of your business, month by month. Your bank account has become your most important financial document.

"The platform has moved the industry from looking at static documents to understanding the actual health of a business in real time. Credit scores and tax returns tell you what happened. Cash flow tells you what's happening."

— From Ned's lender conversations

This matters because it changes what you need to prepare. A good credit score still helps — but it's no longer enough on its own. What lenders want to see is a business that has consistent, predictable income and isn't draining cash faster than it brings it in.

The key shift to understand

Lenders used to ask: "Do you have good credit and something to put up as collateral?"
They now ask: "Does your bank account show a business that can actually repay this loan?"
These are different questions. The rest of this guide is about making sure your answer to the second one is yes.

Not all lenders are the same. A traditional bank, a CDFI (Community Development Financial Institution), and an online lender each look at your business slightly differently. CDFIs and mission lenders tend to be more flexible — they're focused on the health of the community, not just the return — but they're still making a business decision. Understanding which type of lender you're talking to helps you know what to emphasize.

What Lenders Actually Look At

The 6 signals that move most small business loan decisions

When a lender opens your bank statements, they're not reading every line. They're scanning for specific signals — things that either build confidence or raise red flags. Here are the six most important ones, drawn directly from what lenders have told us.

Signal 01

Cash flow consistency

Revenue that shows up regularly and predictably beats revenue that's high but erratic. A lender who sees $8,000 coming in every month feels more confident than one who sees $2,000 one month and $22,000 the next — even if the average is the same.

Signal 02 — Deal killer

NSF history (bounced payments)

NSFs — Non-Sufficient Funds — are one of the fastest ways to end a loan conversation. Even 2-3 in the past 6 months can stop a deal cold. They signal that you're regularly spending more than you have.

Signal 03 — Deal killer

Commingling funds

If your personal and business money live in the same account, it's a serious red flag. Lenders need to see a clean picture of your business's cash flow — mixing accounts makes that impossible and signals disorganization.

Signal 04

Repayment headroom

Lenders calculate whether your business generates enough free cash to make monthly loan payments and still run. If the math doesn't work, they'll either say no or come back with a smaller number than you asked for.

Signal 05

Revenue trajectory

Are you growing, flat, or declining? Growth matters more than the absolute number. A business making $6,000/month and growing is often a better bet than one making $10,000/month and shrinking.

Signal 06 — Trust signal

Willingness to share data

Borrowers who connect their bank accounts and share financial information openly are signaling that they have nothing to hide. Refusing to connect accounts or being slow to provide documents is a yellow flag — sometimes a red one.

"If they've had five NSFs in the last six months, we're done. If they're commingling funds, we're also done."

— Ulric Donawa, BCL of Texas

"We're not pointing to [the data] and saying, this is why we're doing it. But in addition to it, we can see these things. Trust but verify."

— Todd Buchanan, Texas Capital
The practical takeaway

Before you apply for a loan, look at your own bank account the way a lender would. Ask yourself: Are there NSFs? Is business money mixed with personal? Does income show up consistently? If you can see the problems, you can fix them before a lender sees them too.

How to Read Your Own Cash Flow

The numbers lenders will calculate before you even sit down with them

When a lender reviews your application, they run a set of calculations on your bank data. You should run them first. Knowing these numbers before the conversation means you won't be surprised — and you can explain anything unusual before a lender asks.

1
Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

This is the most important calculation a lender runs. It answers: can this business afford to make loan payments? A DSCR above 1.25 is generally considered healthy — it means you have 25% more income than you need to cover your debts.

2
Average daily balance

Lenders look at the average amount sitting in your account day-to-day. A consistently low average balance signals that you're running thin on cash. A healthy average balance signals you're managing money well.

3
Monthly revenue pattern

They're looking at how much comes in each month, whether it's consistent, and whether there are seasonal patterns. They want to understand your "normal" — so anything that looks unusual will need explaining.

4
Free cash flow

After all your regular expenses, how much is left? That's what you have available to repay a loan. Lenders size loans based on this number — which is why you often get approved for less than you asked for.

How DSCR works
DSCR = Monthly Net Income ÷ Monthly Debt Payments

Example: You bring in $8,000/month after expenses. You already have $1,500/month in debt payments. A new loan would add $800/month. Your DSCR would be: $8,000 ÷ ($1,500 + $800) = 3.47 — well above the 1.25 threshold.

On seasonality

If your business has big seasonal swings — a slow winter, a busy summer — explain this proactively before a lender asks. Lenders understand seasonality. What worries them is unexplained volatility. Saying "my revenue drops 40% in January every year but recovers by March" is completely different from having your account look erratic without explanation.

Before You Apply

The practical checklist every borrower should complete before submitting an application

Most loan applications get stuck — or rejected — because of things that could have been fixed before the application was submitted. Here's what to do before you apply. Check each one off as you go.

  • Separate your accounts. Business money and personal money should be in completely different accounts. If they're mixed right now, open a business checking account and start moving your business transactions there. Give it a few months before applying.
  • Have at least 12 months of clean bank history. 24 months is better. The longer your history, the clearer the picture — and the more confident a lender can be.
  • Know your revenue number and be able to defend it. Not from a document — from memory. "My business brings in about $8,000 to $10,000 a month in the busy season and around $5,000 in January and February." That's the kind of answer that builds trust.
  • List your existing debt obligations. Other loans, credit cards, payment plans. Lenders will find them. Knowing them yourself means you can speak to them confidently instead of getting caught off guard.
  • Write a one-paragraph "use of proceeds." What exactly is this money for? "I want to hire a second employee so I can take on more clients" is a use of proceeds. "Working capital" is not. Be specific.
  • Know your business's legal and registration basics. Your legal structure (LLC, sole proprietor, S-corp), your EIN, and where your business is registered. These come up early in every application.
  • Check your account for NSFs. If there are any in the past 6 months, be prepared to explain them. If there are many, it may be worth waiting a few months until your account history is cleaner.

"When you say, send me your financial statement, they send you their bank statement. That's not a character flaw — it's a gap we can fill."

— Todd Buchanan, Texas Capital

What Happens During Underwriting

A behind-the-scenes look at how lenders make decisions

After you submit your application, it goes through a process called underwriting — where a lender analyzes your financial picture and decides whether to approve you, for how much, and at what terms. Most borrowers have no idea what happens in this process. Here's what's actually going on.

1
The credit memo

Someone at the lending organization writes a document summarizing your business, your financials, and their recommendation. This document goes to a loan committee or decision-maker. It's essentially someone telling the story of your business — using your data. You want that story to be easy to tell.

2
The loan committee

Many lenders — especially CDFIs — have a committee that reviews and approves loans. This might be a group of 3-7 people who meet weekly or monthly. They're reading the credit memo, not your application directly. Making the credit memo writer's job easy is one of the most underrated things a borrower can do.

3
Why they come back with a smaller number

If a lender approves you for less than you asked for, it's almost always because their DSCR calculation shows that's the most your cash flow can support. This isn't a rejection — it's a sizing decision. You can often negotiate, or accept the smaller amount and build a track record for a larger loan later.

Slows deals down

Missing or disorganized documents

Every time you have to go back and forth sending documents, the process slows. Prepare everything before you apply.

Kills deals

NSFs, commingling, declining revenue

These are the three most common reasons deals don't move forward. All three are visible in your bank data from the first review.

Speeds deals up

Clean accounts, consistent revenue, proactive communication

Borrowers who are organized, responsive, and forthcoming about their business move through underwriting faster.

Speeds deals up

A clear use of proceeds

Lenders want to understand what the money is for and why it makes sense for your business. A clear, specific answer is one of the fastest ways to build confidence.

How to Present Yourself

The communication skills that close loans faster

Lenders are making a decision about a person, not just a balance sheet. How you communicate — about your business, about your challenges, about what you need and why — matters more than most borrowers realize.

1
Explain your business in 60 seconds

Practice saying out loud: what you do, who you serve, how long you've been doing it, and what the money is for. "I run a cleaning business in Houston, I've been operating for 6 years, and I serve about 40 regular residential clients. I'm looking for $25,000 to hire a second employee so I can take on the commercial clients I've been turning away." That's a good 60 seconds.

2
Talk about a rough stretch without losing the deal

If there's a bad period in your numbers, address it before they ask. "In early 2023, my biggest client cancelled — you'll see a dip in revenue for about four months. I replaced that revenue by the fall." Context turns a red flag into a story. Don't wait for them to find it.

3
What to do when you don't have something they asked for

Don't disappear. Respond quickly, explain what you have and what you don't, and offer what you can. "I don't have audited financial statements, but I can send 24 months of bank statements and my last two tax returns." Responsiveness signals trustworthiness even when the documents aren't perfect.

4
Questions you should be asking your lender

Good borrowers ask questions. "What would make this a stronger application?" "Is there anything in my file that concerns you?" "If you approve a smaller amount, is there a path to the full amount later?" These questions show you understand the process and are serious about the relationship.

The follow-up rule

Follow up once a week if you haven't heard anything. Once is professional. Twice in a day is not. The goal is to stay top of mind without being a burden. A simple message — "Just following up to see if there's anything else you need from me" — keeps the process moving without being annoying.

After the Loan — Staying Loan-Ready

How to make the next loan easier than the first

Getting approved is the beginning, not the end. How you behave after you receive a loan determines whether your lender becomes a long-term partner — and whether getting your next loan is easier or harder than the first.

1
Lenders often monitor your cash flow after funding

Many mission lenders and CDFIs maintain a relationship with their borrowers — not to police them, but because they genuinely want you to succeed. Expect check-ins. Respond to them promptly. Share updates proactively if something changes in your business.

2
Use this loan to build a track record for the next one

Every on-time payment you make is proof that you're a good borrower. Lenders remember. If you want access to larger capital later — or better terms — the foundation is built right now, in how you handle this loan.

3
Tell your lender early if something goes wrong

If you're going to miss a payment or your business hits a rough patch, call your lender before they call you. Lenders are far more willing to work with borrowers who communicate proactively than ones who go silent. Being upfront about a problem is never as damaging as disappearing.

4
Keep your accounts clean — permanently

No commingling. No NSFs. Regular, predictable income. Clean books. These aren't just things to do before a loan application — they're the habits of a business that will always have access to capital when it needs it.

The long game

The borrowers who build the best relationships with lenders aren't necessarily the ones with the highest revenue or the cleanest credit. They're the ones who communicate well, manage their accounts carefully, and treat the lending relationship like what it is: a partnership. That reputation follows you and makes every future conversation easier.
